# Altered State Publishing
<small>Designing Workflows for Alternative Publishing</small>

Students research an exhibition catalog, write a counter-narrative, and design a faux page — published directly into the book. **We love pedagogy, but our systems treat this as damage.** How do we treat it as publishing?

## The Provocation

What happens when an object asks to be read, handled, described, preserved, and circulated, but does not fit the systems built to manage those things? Zines, pamphlets, artist publications, and other small press objects often resist easy classification. They blur the line between publication and artifact, between record and intervention. What assumptions do our systems make about authorship, authority, format, value, and permanence?

## Mini Zine

Create a mini zine about a text that has been challenged, dismissed, debated, or overlooked. Your zine should take a position: defend it, critique it, reinterpret it, or argue why it belongs in a collection. (10 Mins)

Choose a text that is:

- controversial
- widely loved but you disagree with
- widely dismissed but you think matters
- not usually treated as “scholarly”
- personally meaningful and worth preserving

## Anatomy of Your Mini Zine

Here’s a breakdown of the core elements to include in your mini zine, guiding you through the narrative arc of your critical response.

### Page One: Title

The original book's title and author.

### Inside Spread 1: Consensus

What critics, scholars, or the public say about the text.

### Inside Spread 2: Your Voice

Your personal critique, defense, or reinterpretation

### Inside Spread 3: Intervention

One specific thing you would add, change, or challenge in the book.

### Back Cover: Your Mark

Your name, alias, or chosen identifier

## Mini Zine

What you just made is a physical intervention into a field of meaning.

By folding paper, selecting language, adding images, and shaping a small publication, you did more than express an opinion. You entered into relation with other texts in a visible, material way. Your zine does not sit outside scholarship. It interrupts it, extends it, and reframes it. It makes argument tangible.

## The Breakout: Infrastructure Design

In this scenario, library interventions have been created by students, they have brought the items to you along with "Context Cards".  Participants split into two working "Departments" — each handed a physical book with a student insert and a focused design prompt. We will be capturing what we make to add to the toolkit, see the release forms.

### Group 1: The Editors

Policy & Ethics
- Write the "Acceptance Criteria" — who approves what gets inserted?
- Design a peer review process for student-authored content
- Resolve copyright questions around the original text

### Group 2: The Catalogers

Discovery & Metadata

- Spine sticker, MARC record note, or QR code to the IR?
- Make the intervention findable — without erasing the original
- Draft a metadata schema for student interventions

## The Blueprinting: Building Live

### The Process

- Report Out: Groups present key findings
- Blueprinting: Add blueprints to shared workflow
- Toolbox: Assemble guerilla publishing resources

Each group doesn't just share ideas — they add their blueprint directly to a projected, shared digital workflow. The toolbox is built in real time, capturing both outputs and recorded conversation. capture conversation.

### Toolbox Includes

- Policy Drafts: Acceptance criteria and ethics frameworks (Group 1)
- Metadata Schemas: Discovery solutions and cataloging workflows (Group 2)
- Recorded Dialogue: Live conversation captured as part of the toolbox artifact
